Gender Inequity in Financial Vulnerability

Across many countries, women remain disproportionately represented in vulnerable employment. This project explores structural patterns behind gender inequity in labor markets and its implications for financial institutions, policy makers, and economic systems.

Understanding the Key Concepts

Before exploring the visualizations, it is helpful to understand a few key terms used in this project. These concepts explain how employment vulnerability and gender inequities are measured across countries.

Vulnerable Employment

Vulnerable employment refers to jobs where workers have limited job security, unstable income, or limited access to benefits and legal protections. These jobs often include informal work or self-employment without social protections.

Gender Employment Gap

The gender employment gap describes differences between men and women in employment conditions such as job stability, income security, and access to opportunities.

Income Level Groups

Countries are grouped as low-, middle-, or high-income based on economic indicators such as GDP per capita. These categories help compare labor market patterns across different economic contexts.

Why These Patterns Matter for Financial Institutions & Systems

Gender disparities in vulnerable employment are not only a labor issue but they also influence financial inclusion, credit access, and economic stability. The patterns shown in the visualizations above highlight several areas where financial institutions and policy makers can design more equitable financial systems.

Access to Credit

Workers in vulnerable employment often lack stable income documentation, which can make it harder to qualify for traditional loans or credit products. Financial institutions may need alternative credit assessment models to better serve these populations.

Financial Product Design

Women in vulnerable employment may benefit from financial products that support irregular income patterns, such as flexible savings tools, microloans, or adaptive repayment schedules.

Risk Assessment

Understanding employment vulnerability across regions can help financial institutions better assess economic risk and identify underserved markets where financial services could have the greatest impact.

Policy & Economic Development

Governments and development organizations can use this data to design policies that reduce employment vulnerability and improve financial inclusion for women in the workforce.
